Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and Its Relationship with Density Functions and CDFs

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) establishes the profound connection between the derivative of a function and the integral. It states that if \( f \) is a continuous real-valued function defined on an interval \( [a, b] \), and if \( F \) is the indefinite integral of \( f \) on that interval, then:

$$ F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{for all} \quad x \in [a, b] $$

The theorem consists of two main parts. The first part asserts that the derivative of the integral of a function \( f(x) \) is simply the function itself. The second part allows us to compute the definite integral of \( f(x) \) as the difference of the values of \( F(x) \) at the bounds of the interval:

$$ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) $$

Relationship with Probability Density Functions

In probability theory, the probability density function (PDF) \( f(x) \) represents the likelihood that a continuous random variable takes on a particular value. The cumulative distribution function (CDF), denoted \( F(x) \), is related to the PDF through the following integral relationship:

$$ F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt $$

The CDF \( F(x) \) represents the probability that a random variable \( X \) will take a value less than or equal to \( x \). This connection between the PDF and CDF is a direct application of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. Differentiating the CDF with respect to \( x \) yields the PDF:

$$ f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x) $$

Example: Standard Normal Distribution

For the standard normal distribution, the probability density function is expressed as:

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{x^2}{2}} $$

The cumulative distribution function for the standard normal distribution is the integral of the PDF:

$$ F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{t^2}{2}} \, dt $$

By applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we can verify that differentiating the CDF with respect to \( x \) restores the original PDF:

$$ f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{x^2}{2}} $$
