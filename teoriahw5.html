Independence, Uncorrelation, and Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is a fundamental inequality in linear algebra and statistics. It gives an upper bound for the absolute value of the inner product of two vectors in terms of their magnitudes. The inequality is expressed as:

$$| \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle | \leq \| \mathbf{u} \| \cdot \| \mathbf{v} \|$$

Here, \( \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \) is the inner product between vectors \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \), and \( \| \mathbf{u} \| \) and \( \| \mathbf{v} \| \) are their magnitudes (norms).

Simple Proof of Cauchy-Schwarz

Consider a linear combination of \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \) with a scalar parameter \( t \):

$$ \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} - t \mathbf{v} $$

Now, compute the square of the norm of \( \mathbf{w} \):

$$ \| \mathbf{w} \|^2 = \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{w} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{u} - t \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} - t \mathbf{v} \rangle $$

Expanding the inner product and simplifying gives the formula:

$$ \| \mathbf{w} \|^2 = \| \mathbf{u} \|^2 - 2t \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle + t^2 \| \mathbf{v} \|^2 $$

The norm squared must be non-negative, so the quadratic in \( t \) has a minimum value at \( t = \frac{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}{\| \mathbf{v} \|^2} \). Substituting this value of \( t \) into the formula, we get the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

$$ | \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle | \leq \| \mathbf{u} \| \cdot \| \mathbf{v} \| $$

Independence vs Uncorrelation

In probability theory and statistics, independence and uncorrelation are two distinct concepts. Let's explore their differences and measures of dependence.

Independence

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are said to be independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the occurrence of the other. Formally, the joint probability of the two variables is the product of their marginal probabilities:

$$ P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \cdot P(Y) $$

Independence implies that knowing the value of one variable provides no information about the other.

Uncorrelation

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are said to be uncorrelated if their covariance is zero:

$$ \text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] = 0 $$

The covariance is a measure of the linear relationship between two variables. When the covariance is zero, there is no linear relationship between \( X \) and \( Y \). However, uncorrelated variables may still have a non-linear relationship.

Conceptual Differences

- Independence implies that knowing the value of one variable provides no information about the other, in terms of both linear and non-linear relationships. - Uncorrelation only indicates that the linear relationship between variables is absent. It does not imply the absence of a non-linear relationship.
